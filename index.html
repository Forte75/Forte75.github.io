How to fight the recent fentanyl crisis in San Francisco?

By Maria Vendas & Diogo Forte · Social Data Analysis · April 2026

San Francisco is currently undergoing a fentanyl crisis, as many other cities in the United States. A crime data analysis revealed that the city has suffered a previous drug crisis and was able to mitigate the issue. Could we use previous data on drug/narcotics crime to fight this new epidemic?

Section 01

Dataset Overview and Methodology

Every crime leaves a paper trail. For over two decades, San Francisco's Police Department has been quietly logging each one — the what, the where, the when. We took that trail and followed it all the way back to 2003.

22 years of unbroken street-level data, from 2003 to 2025

Two separate datasets, published openly on SF OpenData, cover this span. We stitched them together into a single continuous record — aligned on crime type, date, district, and coordinates — so the story could be told without gaps. The overlap was clean. The picture that emerged was not.

2 datasets merged into one unified timeline — 2003–2018 and 2018–2025

The data spans from over 32 categories between the 2 datasets. However, to ensure consistency across the entire timeline, we focused our analysis on the 11 categories that are present in both datasets.

11 categories assault · warrants · theft · robbery · vandalism · forgery · liquor laws · prostitution · suicide · drugs

Geographically, the city is divided into 10 police districts. Each has its own character, its own rhythms — and its own relationship with the drug crisis. None more so than the Tenderloin, which sits at the epicentre of what is now San Francisco's most urgent public health emergency.

10 districts Central · Southern · Bayview · Mission · Park · Richmond · Ingleside · Taraval · Northern · Tenderloin

Key finding

Most offenses bottomed out during 2019–2021 — but narcotics are the exception. We are seeing a relentless, exponential increase that mirrors the crisis of 2005–2010.
